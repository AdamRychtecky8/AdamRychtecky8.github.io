Dialed — Mental Performance Platform for Golfers

Founder · Full-Stack Engineer — built solo using Claude, Copilot, and GPT-4o

Most golfers who want to improve their mental game can’t access real coaching — Dialed brings evidence-based mental performance training to anyone with a phone. Built the full stack solo: PostgreSQL schema with RLS as a hard data boundary, server-first Next.js with React Server Components, timezone-aware daily logic, TypeScript strict mode throughout, and canonical registries enforcing single source of truth across the data model, UI, and analytics layer.

Core product is functional and in active alpha: 5-factor daily check-in feeding a rule-based 8-state headspace classifier, 15+ evidence-based drills with an explicit state machine runner and OpenAI TTS voice guidance, SHA256-hashed server-side audio caching to Supabase Storage, post-round reflection, and streak tracking. Every check-in is an immutable labeled time series record and every round reflection is a before/after pair — the data model was built from the start to support ML-driven personalization. Coach and Caddie AI agents fully designed with specs and stubs — Phase 2.

Ensemble Decision-Making Under Correlation: Humans vs. LLM Groups

Honors thesis research · UCSB Vision & Image Understanding Lab

Replicated and extended Juni & Eckstein (2015) across 507,600 trials, comparing human groups and LLM ensembles (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini) on a 2AFC visual detection task. Groups of 12 outperform individuals by up to 15% — but gains plateau at n≈7, explained by high error correlation in 35–40% of model pairs.

Modeling Perceptual Decisions: Humans vs. LLMs

PSTAT 231 Final Project — class project using real honors thesis data

Predicted whether humans and LLMs would respond “present” or “absent” on identical visual detection trials — modeling agent behavior, not ground truth. Fit a GAM as an interpretable baseline and CatBoost to capture feature interactions the additive model cannot. SHAP analysis revealed the sharpest human–LLM difference: LLMs treat trial clarity as a reliable cue; humans show almost no sensitivity to the same signal.

PM2.5 and Alzheimer’s Disease: Mechanisms, Risk, and Economic Burden

Undergraduate systematic literature review — WRIT W109ST, UCSB

A systematic review synthesizes the existing literature on a focused question by applying a reproducible search protocol, explicit inclusion criteria, and a classification scheme — rather than selectively reading whatever turns up. This review applied that process to 12 peer-reviewed studies (UCSB Library and PubMed, 2015–2024) to address three research questions: (1) the biological mechanisms through which PM2.5 contributes to AD onset or progression, (2) the public health implications of the PM2.5–AD relationship and what they suggest for policy, and (3) the economic cost to society.

On the biological side, a controlled mouse study found neuronal loss and elevated phosphorylated-tau in the olfactory bulb after three months of PM2.5 exposure, with neurodegenerative changes following the nasal entry and spread route — but no amyloid pathology, likely due to the short exposure window. A Mexico City study using post-mortem tissue from children and teens found amyloid-β deposits and cerebrovascular inflammation in high-pollution subjects, consistent with the Amyloid Cascade Hypothesis. Epidemiologically, a Canadian cohort study found proximity to major roads correlated with AD hazard ratios ranging from 0.998 (furthest) to 2.05 (closest, 95% CI). A seasonal study in China found winter pro-apoptotic gene expression 2.19× the clean-air control. Economically, Sweden attributed 820 AD cases per year (5% of national total) to PM2.5, at an average lifetime cost of €213,000 per case; a Sichuan Province study attributed 16.48% of AD hospitalizations to PM2.5, with a documented economic cost of $2.56 million USD. Averaging the two attribution estimates suggests ~10.7% of AD cases globally may be PM2.5-related.
